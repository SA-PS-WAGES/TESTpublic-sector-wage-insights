Financial Position

PSA financial position in the last financial year

At a basic level, a trade union should cover day-to-day operating costs from member dues. When that doesn’t happen, the incentive structure shifts toward safer options.

Key financial insights

Snapshot

Coverage gap

Dues lagged operating costs.

Investment income filled the hole, yet the year still closed in loss.

Incentive tilt

Tight budgets reward safe wins.

Recruitment can feel more reliable than high-stakes bargaining outcomes.

Capitation cap

ASO5 limits income growth.

Above the cap, wage gains don’t grow upstream revenue the same way.

Operating coverage and the gap

Budget basics

Membership dues should fund the baseline union operations that keep members supported.

Core costs normally include:

  • Organising and member support
  • Admin and office operations
  • Legal representation
  • Campaigning and advocacy
What we know

Dues did not fully cover operating costs.

Investment income bridged the gap, yet PSA still finished the year in deficit.

How dues and capitation shape incentives

Funding flow

Members pay dues as a percentage of wages. That revenue is split, which shapes the choices leaders make.

  • Some stays with PSA to run day-to-day operations.
  • Some flows upward as capitation.

Important clarification:

  • Members do not pay capitation directly.
  • PSA pays capitation from the dues members already pay.
Incentive snapshot

More members bring immediate revenue.

Higher wages increase revenue only if the bargaining fight is won.

When finances are tight, chasing headcount can feel like the safer option.

The ASO5 cap and why it matters

Structural pressure

There is another structural lever that quietly shapes priorities.

If capitation is capped at ASO5:

  • Pay rises above ASO5 do not increase federal income.
  • Every additional member still does.

Resulting signal

The system rewards membership growth.

Once the cap is reached, outcomes compete with recruitment for scarce attention.

This doesn’t imply bad faith. It shows how incentives can pull strategy in one direction.

What members have experienced

On the ground

From this bargaining round, many members have observed:

  • Limited support for industrial action across the majority.
  • Modest outcomes framed as wins.
  • Recruitment pressure pushed onto existing members.
  • Low density blamed when results fall short.
  • Frustration redirected into “next time” messaging.
Member takeaway

Pressure is being managed, not amplified.

Momentum stalls when members feel risk is absorbed instead of backed.

What strong organising usually looks like

Comparison

Strong union strategy tends to involve:

  • Backing members willing to take lawful action.
  • Using disruption strategically to build leverage.
  • Being clear and consistent about goals.
  • Owning decisions and outcomes.
  • Growing membership off the back of success.

Impact

Confidence builds when wins are visible.

That visibility compounds bargaining power and long-term engagement.

Why members see HR-style risk management

Perception gap

Corporate HR functions are designed to:

  • Manage legal and reputational risk.
  • Minimise disruption.
  • Control costs.
  • Use careful language to shape expectations.
  • Absorb pressure into process.

Some behaviours members report feel closer to that approach:

  • Risk avoidance taking priority.
  • Disruption treated as something to limit.
  • Careful wording lowering expectations.
  • Accountability flowing downward.

A trade union’s role should be different.

A union exists to take risk on behalf of members, not pass it back to them.

The missed strategic opportunity

Levers

A stronger outcome this round would have helped members and strengthened the union.

Stronger outcomes mean:

  • Higher wages, so the same percentage brings in more revenue.
  • Operating costs better covered by dues.
  • Members stay because faith is restored.
  • New members join because they can see the value.
Two levers

Outcomes and capitation.

Higher wages increase value. More members increase volume.

Together, that strengthens influence and representation at a federal level.

People don’t leave unions that deliver. They leave unions that manage decline.

Where this leaves members

Accountability

Members didn’t fail PSA. Strategic choices were made.

Those choices appear to have prioritised:

  • Risk avoidance over leverage.
  • Stability over outcomes.
  • Numbers over member value.

Why it matters

Members are asking for transparency.

A not-for-profit trade union should be accountable to members and set up to fight for them.

Asking for review is part of union democracy.

Union Reform • Accountability

Union Reform

Across the salaried public sector, many members are beginning to ask a simple but reasonable question: Why does the bargaining strategy, industrial focus, and internal power structure of the PSA appear to prioritise a minority cohort, while larger workforces experience weaker outcomes and limited mobilisation?

This is not about personalities or internal factions. It is about structure, proportionality, and whether the union is operating in the interests of its full membership base.

Representation

How representation is currently structured

Under the current PSA structure, members are grouped into electorates and represented through electorate-based Branch Councillors and an Executive Council elected from within those councillors. In principle, this system is designed to scale representation in line with membership numbers and ensure fairness across diverse workforces. In practice, however, the way electorates are grouped — particularly within the OPS stream — has created a concentration of representation that does not reflect the broader salaried workforce.

OPS Electorate

The OPS electorate and disproportionate influence

Correctional Officers are currently grouped within the OPS electorate, despite being a highly concentrated workforce largely contained within a single department (DCS) and operating under unique industrial conditions that differ from most OPS members. As a result:

  • All OPS councillor positions are held by DCS-based Correctional Officers.
  • This concentration has flowed through to Executive Council representation.
  • Strategic priorities have increasingly aligned with Correctional Officer bargaining objectives.

This has occurred not because of wrongdoing, but because the structure itself allows it.

Democratic fairness

Why this matters for democratic fairness

When a single, highly organised cohort dominates an electorate:

  • Other OPS members lose meaningful representation.
  • Broader OPS priorities struggle to surface.
  • Executive decision-making risks reflecting density, not scale.

Meanwhile, large salaried workforces — particularly ASO and Health-based roles — are fragmented across electorates despite having higher total headcount, greater public-facing impact, and significantly stronger industrial leverage. This raises legitimate questions about whether electorate grouping is still fit for purpose.

Industrial leverage

Industrial strategy and leverage: a mismatch

Recent industrial action highlights a deeper issue.

  • Correctional Officers undertook prolonged industrial action.
  • They experienced limited public or political pressure.
  • Outcomes were modest relative to effort.

By contrast, larger salaried workforces include:

  • Hospital ward clerks and outpatient staff.
  • SAPOL and Service SA customer service officers.
  • Front-facing administrative and operational roles across government.

These roles directly affect service delivery, carry higher public visibility, and represent a far stronger industrial lever. Yet they were not mobilised at scale, and in some cases, were actively discouraged from action. Members are entitled to ask why.

Intent

This is not about smear or blame

Members are not seeking reform through accusation or internal division. This inquiry is grounded in good governance:

  • Transparency.
  • Proportional representation.
  • Ethical bargaining strategy.
  • Accountability to stated commitments.

The question is not who is in leadership. The question is whether the system itself is producing fair outcomes.

Commitments

Why commitments matter

During the PSA Change campaign, members were presented with clear commitments across classification reform, wage growth, separate agreements for distinct cohorts, and improved conditions and allowances. These commitments were decisive for many voters.

If circumstances have changed, members deserve clear outcomes, evidence-based explanations, and justification for any shift in position. That is the foundation of trust in a democratic organisation.

Campaign promises

Commitments members are asking to be accounted for

The following commitments were publicly made during the PSA Change campaign and form the basis of this inquiry:

  • Improved classification streams and new increments across OPS, ASO, CO and PO classifications.
  • OPS1 to be trainee-only, with new increments for OPS2 and OPS3.
  • New increments for CO3, PO3, ASO2, ASO3 and ASO6.
  • Automatic progression from CO2 to CO3 on completion of Certificate IV.
  • Operational Supervisor classification at CO5 (not CO4).
  • Separate enterprise agreement for Allied Health Professionals.
  • Separate enterprise agreement for Correctional Officers and ICU.
  • Up to 20% wage increase in the first 12 months, backdated to October 2024.
  • Conversion to permanency clause.
  • Six weeks annual leave for seven-day roster workers.
  • Saturday penalties at 175% and Sunday penalties at 200%.
  • Additional paid leave for regional employees.
  • Regional incentives allowance.
  • Paid parental leave reforms.
  • Two weeks paid partner leave.
  • Reproductive leave.
  • Seven days paid wellbeing leave for specified agencies.
  • 20% Officer-in-Charge allowance.
  • Improved on-call and recall provisions (annualised allowance).
  • Health-site parking caps or free public transport access.
  • A formal offer received within a reasonable timeframe before the ballot opens or;
  • The decision to ballot rescinded and moved to a separate SGM to decide whether to resume bargaining.

Members are simply asking: what was delivered, what wasn’t, and why?

Bigger picture

The bigger picture

  • No minority cohort holds structural dominance.
  • No electorate is functionally silenced.
  • Industrial strategy reflects real leverage.
  • Commitments are honoured or transparently explained.

Ultimately, this is about restoring confidence that the PSA is a member-controlled union — in practice, not just in name.

Requisition

How the requisition process works

To formally request transparency and disclosure, members are using a Special General Meeting (SGM) requisition in accordance with PSA rules.

  • Only current, financial PSA members are eligible to sign the requisition.
  • Each signature must be genuine and provided by the member themselves.
  • Once a meaningful number of signatures has been collected, members are encouraged to coordinate with trusted colleagues or existing member groups (where you are already connected) to consolidate signed requisition pages.
  • Consolidated requisitions can then be submitted together to ensure they are formally recognised and progressed in line with PSA governance processes.

This approach ensures the request is:

  • Member-led.
  • Rule-compliant.
  • Transparent.
  • Collective, not individual.

The objective is not confrontation, but to enable members to make informed decisions based on full and accurate information.

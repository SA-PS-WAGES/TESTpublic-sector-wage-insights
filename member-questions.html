The Member's Union

Commitments, Claims, and Democratic Process

The current offer — and why members are asking questions

Context

Why members are asking questions

As the current enterprise bargaining offer stands, many members consider that it does not deliver on the majority of commitments made during the PSA Change campaign, nor on the log of claims as those commitments were communicated to the broader membership.

As a result, members are raising reasonable questions about process, transparency, and democratic integrity.

Members are asking

Key questions about the process

  • If this offer does not deliver on the majority of the log of claims as it was communicated to members, how did it move from negotiation between the government and PSA, to Council approval, and then to ballot?
  • Why was a formal offer not presented to workers before the ballot opened?
  • What information was available to Council when the decision to proceed to ballot was made?

These questions are not about personalities, factions, or internal disputes. They relate to member control, informed consent, and good governance.

SGM request

What members are doing about it

Members are using the rules of the union to request a Special General Meeting (SGM) so these questions can be examined transparently and lawfully.

Campaign commitments

PSA Change commitments members want accounted for

During the PSA Change campaign, members were presented with clear and specific commitments. These commitments informed how many members voted and the mandate they believed they were supporting.

Members are now asking for clarity on what has been delivered, what has not, and the reasons for any changes or omissions.

The following commitments were publicly made:

  1. Improved classification streams and new increments across OPS, ASO, CO and PO classifications.
  2. OPS1 to be trainee-only, with new increments for OPS2 and OPS3.
  3. New increments for CO3, PO3, ASO2, ASO3 and ASO6.
  4. Automatic progression from CO2 to CO3 on completion of Certificate IV.
  5. Operational Supervisor classification at CO5 (not CO4).
  6. Separate enterprise agreement for Correctional Officers and ICU.
  7. Up to 20% wage increase in the first 12 months, backdated to October 2024.
  8. Conversion to permanency clause.
  9. Six weeks annual leave for seven-day roster workers.
  10. Saturday penalties at 175% and Sunday penalties at 200%.
  11. Additional paid leave for regional employees.
  12. Regional incentives allowance.
  13. Paid parental leave reforms.
  14. Two weeks paid partner leave.
  15. Reproductive leave.
  16. Seven days paid wellbeing leave for specified agencies.
  17. 20% Officer-in-Charge allowance.
  18. Improved on-call and recall provisions (annualised allowance).
  19. Health-site parking caps or free public transport access.
  20. Reduction of membership fees, funded by significantly cutting the General Secretary’s wage.
  21. A formal offer received within a reasonable timeframe before the ballot opens.

Members are not suggesting that all commitments must be achieved immediately. They are seeking accountability and transparency against what was promised.

Requisition process

How the SGM requisition process works

To formally request transparency and review, members are using the SGM requisition process under the PSA rulebook.

Key points:

  • Only current, financial PSA members are eligible to sign the requisition.
  • Each signature must be genuine and provided by the member themselves.
  • Members may coordinate with trusted colleagues or existing member groups to consolidate signed requisition pages.
  • Consolidated requisitions can be submitted together so they are formally recognised and progressed in accordance with PSA rules.

This is a lawful, established, member-led process. Its purpose is to ensure members are able to make informed decisions, based on full and accurate information.

Future-proofing

Future-proofing democracy in the union

Members are also raising broader questions about how the union’s democratic structures operate and how they may need to adapt over time.

OPS representation and balance

Correctional Officers and DCS operational members are a large and well-organised cohort and are entitled to representation that reflects their membership size. At the same time, OPS members work across many departments, and OPS membership is likely to increase if PSA achieves strong bargaining outcomes.

Some members are therefore proposing that:

  • Representation arrangements ensure Correctional Officers and DCS operational members are represented appropriately without unintentionally concentrating influence within a single OPS electorate.
  • OPS members from other departments are able to gain fair and proportional representation.
  • Representation can scale over time so that new members are not structurally disadvantaged.

These discussions are about structure, balance, and long-term fairness, not about blame or exclusion.

Why now

Why members are raising this now

Members are seeking:

  • Clear and accessible information.
  • Transparent decision-making.
  • Fair and proportional representation.
  • Confidence that union processes reflect member interests.

The SGM requisition process exists to support exactly these objectives.
